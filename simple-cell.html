🔋 What is a Simple Cell?

A simple cell converts chemical energy into electrical energy using two different metals

🔑 Key Definition

A simple chemical cell (also called a voltaic cell or galvanic cell) is a device that converts chemical energy into electrical energy. It consists of:

1. Two different metals (electrodes)
2. An electrolyte (a solution that can conduct electricity)
3. An external wire connecting the two metals (with a voltmeter to measure voltage)

The more reactive metal becomes the negative terminal. It loses electrons (oxidation) and dissolves.

Positive Terminal

The less reactive metal becomes the positive terminal. It receives electrons. H₂ may form on its surface.

📏

Voltage

The greater the difference in reactivity between the two metals, the higher the voltage produced.

💡 Key difference from electrolysis: In electrolysis, electricity is used to cause a chemical reaction (electrical → chemical). In a simple cell, a chemical reaction produces electricity (chemical → electrical). They are reverse processes!

🔬 How a Simple Cell Works

The classic Zn-Cu cell explained step by step

⚡ Zinc–Copper Simple Cell

1. Zinc Dissolves (Oxidation)

Zinc is more reactive than copper. Zn atoms on the surface lose 2 electrons and dissolve into the solution as Zn²⁺ ions. The zinc electrode gradually gets smaller.

At Zinc (Negative Terminal) Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻   [Oxidation]

2. Electrons Flow Through Wire

The electrons released by zinc travel through the external wire (and through the voltmeter) from the zinc to the copper electrode. This flow of electrons = electric current.

3. Hydrogen Ions Gain Electrons (Reduction)

At the copper electrode, H⁺ ions from the acidic electrolyte gain the electrons that arrived through the wire. They are reduced to form hydrogen gas, which appears as bubbles on the copper surface.

At Copper (Positive Terminal) 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g)   [Reduction]

4. Voltmeter Reads the Voltage

The voltmeter shows the potential difference (voltage) between the two metals. For Zn-Cu, this is approximately 1.10 V. The greater the difference in reactivity, the higher the voltage.

✅ Pattern: The voltage increases as the difference in reactivity between the two metals increases. Using two metals that are far apart in the reactivity series gives the highest voltage. Using the same metal gives 0 V.

⚡ Simple Cell vs Electrolysis

Two opposite processes — understand the key differences

Feature Simple Cell (Voltaic Cell) Electrolysis (Electrolytic Cell)
Energy conversion Chemical → Electrical Electrical → Chemical
Power source? Produces electricity (IS the source) Requires external power supply
Reaction type Spontaneous (happens on its own) Non-spontaneous (forced by electricity)
Electrodes Two different metals Usually same material (carbon/platinum)
Negative electrode More reactive metal (anode in chemistry terms) Cathode (connected to − terminal)
Positive electrode Less reactive metal (cathode in chemistry terms) Anode (connected to + terminal)
Electron flow From more reactive → less reactive metal From battery → cathode; anode → battery
Voltmeter reading Shows a positive voltage Not applicable (uses ammeter)
Example Zn-Cu cell, batteries Electrolysis of CuSO₄, NaCl
⚠️ Common exam confusion: In a simple cell, the more reactive metal is the negative terminal. Don't confuse this with electrolysis where the cathode is negative and is connected to the battery's negative terminal. In a simple cell, there IS no external battery — the cell is the battery!

🧪 Factors Affecting the Simple Cell

What changes the voltage and how long the cell lasts

📈 Higher Voltage

Use metals with a greater difference in reactivity. E.g., Mg-Cu gives higher voltage than Fe-Cu.

⏱️ Cell Life

The cell stops working when the more reactive metal fully dissolves or the electrolyte is used up.

🚫 Zero Voltage

If both electrodes are the same metal, no voltage is produced — there's no difference in reactivity to drive electron flow.

💧 Electrolyte

The electrolyte must be able to conduct electricity (ionic solution or acid). Distilled water or oil will not work.

🔬 Try It: Lemon/Potato Battery

A fun way to demonstrate simple cells at home or in the lab!

  1. Get a lemon (or potato) — this acts as the electrolyte (contains citric/phosphoric acid).
  2. Insert a zinc nail (or galvanised nail) into one side — this is the negative terminal.
  3. Insert a copper coin (or copper wire) into the other side — this is the positive terminal.
  4. Connect a voltmeter to both metals using crocodile clip wires.
  5. Read the voltage! You should get approximately 0.8–1.0 V.
  6. Connect multiple lemon cells in series (+ to −) to increase the total voltage and power a small LED!
📝 Why it works: Zinc is more reactive than copper. Zinc atoms lose electrons (Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻) which flow through the wire to the copper, where H⁺ ions from the lemon's citric acid gain the electrons (2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂).

🎯 Key Exam Points — Simple Cell

The more reactive metal is always the negative terminal. It has a greater tendency to lose electrons and undergo oxidation. Use the reactivity series to determine which metal is more reactive. The metal higher up the series = negative terminal.

Electrons flow from the more reactive metal (−) through the external wire to the less reactive metal (+). This is the opposite direction to conventional current flow. Inside the solution, ions carry the current.

If both electrodes are the same metal, there is no difference in reactivity, so there is no driving force for electrons to flow. The voltmeter reads 0 V.

Use two metals that are further apart in the reactivity series. For example, replace Fe with Mg in a Fe-Cu cell to get a Mg-Cu cell, which has a much higher voltage (2.71 V vs 0.78 V). You can also connect multiple cells in series.

The cell stops when the more reactive metal is completely dissolved (used up), or when the electrolyte is exhausted. Additionally, hydrogen gas bubbles may coat the less reactive electrode (polarisation), reducing efficiency.

The electrolyte completes the circuit internally. While electrons flow through the external wire, ions flow through the electrolyte to maintain electrical neutrality. Without an electrolyte (or with a non-conducting liquid), the cell will not work.
