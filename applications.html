⛏️ Purification of Copper

Impure copper from mining is refined to over 99.99% purity using electrolysis

🔑 Why Purify Copper?

Copper extracted from its ore contains impurities such as zinc, iron, silver, and gold. For electrical wiring, copper must be extremely pure (>99.99%) because impurities increase electrical resistance.

⚙️ The Electrolysis Setup

⊖ Cathode (−)
Thin strip of PURE copper
This is where pure copper will be deposited
🧪 Electrolyte
Copper(II) sulphate solution
CuSO₄(aq) — provides Cu²⁺ ions
⊕ Anode (+)
Block of IMPURE copper
Contains impurities (Zn, Fe, Ag, Au)

🏆 What Happens to Impurities?

Less reactive metals (Ag, Au): Do NOT dissolve. They fall as "anode sludge" — valuable!

More reactive metals (Zn, Fe): Dissolve as ions but are NOT deposited at cathode (too reactive). They stay in solution.

✨ Electroplating

Coating an object with a thin layer of metal for protection, appearance, or corrosion resistance

🔑 What is Electroplating?

Electroplating uses electrolysis to coat a cheaper metal object with a thin layer of a more attractive or resistant metal, such as silver, gold, chromium, or nickel.

📐 The Three Rules of Electroplating Setup

1. CATHODE = Object to be plated

Metal must be deposited onto it (reduction).

2. ANODE = The plating metal

This dissolves to keep the concentration of metal ions constant.

3. ELECTROLYTE = Solution of plating metal ions

E.g., silver nitrate for silver plating.

💡 Memory trick: The plating metal appears in TWO places: as the anode AND in the electrolyte. The object is always the cathode.

🥄 Example: Silver-Plating a Steel Spoon

⊖ Cathode (−)
Steel spoon
Object to be plated
🧪 Electrolyte
Silver nitrate — AgNO₃(aq)
Contains Ag⁺ ions of plating metal
⊕ Anode (+)
Pure silver
Plating metal — dissolves to replace Ag⁺

✅ Reasons for Electroplating

  • Protects against corrosion and rusting
  • Provides an attractive finish
  • Makes objects look like expensive metals at lower cost
  • Increases hardness and durability
  • Creates food-safe surfaces (tin-plating)

❌ Limitations

  • Plating layer is thin — can wear off
  • If scratched, underlying metal can corrode
  • Uses electricity (energy cost)
  • Some electrolytes contain toxic chemicals
  • Requires careful control of current and time

🔄 Metal Transfer by Electrolysis

Understanding how metal moves from the anode to the cathode

🔑 What is Metal Transfer?

When an active (non-inert) metal anode is used, the anode dissolves into the solution as ions, and those ions are then deposited at the cathode. Metal is physically transferred from anode → cathode.

🔄 Metal Transfer — Visual Animation

✅ Conditions for Metal Transfer

  • The anode is made of an active (non-inert) metal
  • The electrolyte contains ions of the same metal
  • The metal ion is preferentially discharged at the cathode
  • A direct current is passed through
