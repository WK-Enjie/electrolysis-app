Q1 MOLTEN [4 marks]
Molten lead(II) bromide, PbBr₂, is electrolysed using carbon electrodes.
  • (a) Why must PbBr₂ be melted before electrolysis? [1]
    In the solid state, ions are held in a fixed lattice and cannot move. When melted, the ions become free to move and can carry charge to the electrodes, allowing the electrolyte to conduct electricity.
  • (b) State what is observed at each electrode. [2]
    Cathode (−): A silvery-grey molten metal (lead) is deposited / collects at the bottom.
    Anode (+): Reddish-brown fumes (bromine gas) are produced.
  • (c) Write the half-equation at the cathode. [1]
    Pb²⁺(l) + 2e⁻ → Pb(l)   [Reduction]
Q2 AQUEOUS [6 marks]
An aqueous solution of copper(II) sulphate, CuSO₄, is electrolysed using carbon electrodes.
  • (a) List ALL ions present in the solution. [2]
    From CuSO₄: Cu²⁺ and SO₄²⁻
    From water: H⁺ and OH⁻
    (Four ions total)
  • (b) Predict and explain the product at the cathode. [2]
    Copper (Cu) is deposited. Cu²⁺ is lower than H⁺ in the electrochemical series (Cu is less reactive than hydrogen), so Cu²⁺ is preferentially discharged.
    Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)
  • (c) Explain why the blue colour of the solution fades over time. [2]
    The blue colour is due to Cu²⁺ ions. As electrolysis proceeds, Cu²⁺ ions are discharged at the cathode and deposited as copper metal. Since the anode is inert (carbon), it does not dissolve, so no new Cu²⁺ ions are added to replace those removed. The concentration of Cu²⁺ decreases, and the blue colour fades.
Q3 APPLICATION [5 marks]
A manufacturer wants to silver-plate a steel spoon using electrolysis.
  • (a) State what should be used as: (i) cathode, (ii) anode, (iii) electrolyte. [3]
    (i) Cathode: The steel spoon (object to be plated)
    (ii) Anode: A piece of pure silver (plating metal)
    (iii) Electrolyte: Silver nitrate solution, AgNO₃(aq) (contains Ag⁺ ions)
  • (b) Write the half-equations at each electrode. [2]
    Cathode: Ag⁺(aq) + e⁻ → Ag(s)  (Reduction — silver deposited on spoon)
    Anode: Ag(s) → Ag⁺(aq) + e⁻  (Oxidation — silver anode dissolves)
Q4 SIMPLE CELL [5 marks]
A simple cell is set up using a zinc plate and a copper plate dipped in dilute sulphuric acid, connected by a wire with a voltmeter.
  • (a) Which metal is the negative terminal? Explain why. [2]
    Zinc is the negative terminal because it is more reactive than copper (higher in the reactivity series). It has a greater tendency to lose electrons and be oxidised.
  • (b) State the direction of electron flow and write the equation at the zinc electrode. [2]
    Electrons flow from zinc (−) to copper (+) through the external wire.
    Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻  (Oxidation)
  • (c) Suggest how to increase the voltage reading. [1]
    Replace zinc with a more reactive metal (e.g., magnesium) to increase the difference in reactivity between the two electrodes, which increases the voltage.
Q5 FUEL CELL [5 marks]
Hydrogen fuel cells are being developed as an alternative to petrol engines in cars.
  • (a) Write the overall equation for the reaction in a hydrogen fuel cell. [1]
    2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)
  • (b) State two advantages of using hydrogen fuel cells instead of petrol engines. [2]
    1. The only product is water — no CO₂ or pollutant gases are produced (environmentally friendly).
    2. Fuel cells are more efficient — they convert chemical energy directly to electrical energy without heat losses.
  • (c) State two disadvantages of hydrogen fuel cells. [2]
    1. Hydrogen is difficult to store safely — it is highly flammable and needs high-pressure tanks.
    2. Fuel cells are expensive because they require platinum catalysts. Also, hydrogen production from fossil fuels still produces CO₂.
