Electrolysis of Molten Ionic Compounds

Understand how molten electrolytes are decomposed by direct current. Explore PbBr₂, NaCl, and Al₂O₃ with step-by-step visuals.

Home › Topic 1: Molten Compounds

🤔 Why Must the Compound Be Molten?

Ionic compounds must be melted (or dissolved) for electrolysis to work. Here's why.

🔑 Key Concept: Ions Must Be Free to Move

In the solid state, ions are held in a fixed lattice structure by strong electrostatic forces. They cannot move to the electrodes, so solid ionic compounds do not conduct electricity.

When melted (molten), the lattice breaks down and ions become free to move. They can now carry charge and migrate to the electrodes when a voltage is applied.

💡 Remember: For electrolysis to occur, there must be: (1) an electrolyte with free-moving ions, (2) two electrodes (usually carbon/graphite or platinum), (3) a direct current (d.c.) power supply.

🔬 Electrolysis of Molten Lead(II) Bromide

PbBr₂ is the classic O-Level example. Follow the step-by-step animation below.

🧪 Electrolyte: Molten PbBr₂
🔌 Electrodes: Carbon (graphite)
🌡️ Temperature: 373°C (m.p.)

⚡ Interactive Electrolysis of Molten PbBr₂

Step 1: Setup

The crucible contains solid PbBr₂. Heat is applied to melt it above its melting point (373°C). Two carbon (graphite) electrodes are dipped into the molten compound and connected to a d.c. power supply.

👀 Observations

⊖ CATHODE (−)

At the Negative Electrode

🪙 Silvery grey molten metal (lead) deposited

Pb²⁺ ions are attracted to the cathode. Each Pb²⁺ ion gains 2 electrons (reduction) to form lead metal atoms that collect at the bottom of the crucible.

Half-equation (Reduction) Pb²⁺(l) + 2e⁻ → Pb(l)
⊕ ANODE (+)

At the Positive Electrode

🟤 Reddish-brown fumes (bromine gas) produced

Br⁻ ions are attracted to the anode. Each Br⁻ ion loses 1 electron (oxidation) to form bromine atoms, which pair up to form Br₂ gas.

Half-equation (Oxidation) 2Br⁻(l) → Br₂(g) + 2e⁻
Overall Equation PbBr₂(l) → Pb(l) + Br₂(g)
📝 Exam tip: Notice the equation shows decomposition — one compound breaking into its elements. This is the key idea of electrolysis: using electricity to decompose a compound.

📝 Step-by-Step Process

Follow each step of the electrolysis of molten PbBr₂

1. Melting the Electrolyte

Solid PbBr₂ is heated in a crucible until it melts (above 373°C). The strong ionic bonds in the lattice are overcome, and the Pb²⁺ and Br⁻ ions become free to move.

2. Connecting the Circuit

Two carbon (graphite) electrodes are placed into the molten PbBr₂ and connected to a d.c. power supply. The electrode connected to the negative terminal is the cathode. The electrode connected to the positive terminal is the anode.

3. Ion Migration

When the current flows:
• Pb²⁺ cations (positive) migrate towards the cathode (−)
• Br⁻ anions (negative) migrate towards the anode (+)
Opposite charges attract!

4. Discharge at Cathode (Reduction)

At the cathode, each Pb²⁺ ion gains 2 electrons from the electrode and is reduced to form a lead atom. Molten lead collects at the bottom of the crucible.

Cathode Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb

5. Discharge at Anode (Oxidation)

At the anode, each Br⁻ ion loses 1 electron to the electrode and is oxidised. Two bromine atoms combine to form a Br₂ molecule, released as reddish-brown gas.

Anode 2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻

6. Electron Flow Completes the Circuit

Electrons flow from the battery → cathode (given to Pb²⁺ ions), and from the anode → battery (received from Br⁻ ions). Inside the electrolyte, ions carry the current. In the external wire, electrons carry the current.

🧂 Electrolysis of Molten Sodium Chloride (NaCl)

Molten NaCl contains Na⁺ cations and Cl⁻ anions that are free to move.

🧪 Electrolyte: Molten NaCl
🔌 Electrodes: Carbon (inert)
🌡️ M.P.: 801°C

⊖ Cathode (−)

  • Ion attracted: Na⁺ (cation)
  • Process: Reduction (gain of electrons)
  • Product: Sodium metal (Na)
  • Observation: Silvery molten metal formed
Cathode Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na

⊕ Anode (+)

  • Ion attracted: Cl⁻ (anion)
  • Process: Oxidation (loss of electrons)
  • Product: Chlorine gas (Cl₂)
  • Observation: Greenish-yellow gas with pungent smell
Anode 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻
Overall Equation 2NaCl(l) → 2Na(l) + Cl₂(g)
⚠️ Safety note: Sodium is a very reactive metal that reacts violently with water and air. Chlorine gas is toxic. This process must be carried out in controlled industrial conditions (the Downs process).

⛏️ Electrolysis of Molten Aluminium Oxide (Al₂O₃)

This is the industrial extraction of aluminium — one of the most important applications of electrolysis! Aluminium cannot be extracted by carbon reduction because it is too reactive.

Why Use Cryolite (Na₃AlF₆)?

Pure Al₂O₃ has a very high melting point of 2072°C, making it extremely expensive to melt. Al₂O₃ is dissolved in molten cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) to lower the melting point to about 950°C, saving energy and costs.

🧪 Electrolyte: Al₂O₃ in molten cryolite
🔌 Electrodes: Carbon (graphite)
🌡️ Temperature: ~950°C

🏭 The Hall-Héroult Cell

⊖ Cathode (Carbon Lining)

  • Ion: Al³⁺
  • Process: Reduction
  • Product: Aluminium metal (sinks to bottom)
Cathode Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al

⊕ Anode (Carbon Rods)

  • Ion: O²⁻
  • Process: Oxidation
  • Product: Oxygen gas (reacts with carbon anode)
Anode 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻
Overall Equation 2Al₂O₃(l) → 4Al(l) + 3O₂(g)
⚠️ Why must the carbon anodes be replaced regularly?
At the high temperature (~950°C), the oxygen gas produced at the anode reacts with the carbon electrodes to form CO₂:

C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)

This gradually burns away the carbon anodes, so they must be replaced periodically. This is a major running cost.
  • Aluminium extraction is very expensive due to the large amount of electricity needed
  • Cryolite lowers the melting point, reducing energy costs
  • Aluminium plants are often built near cheap hydroelectric power sources
  • Recycling aluminium uses only ~5% of the energy needed for extraction

🔬 Electrolysis of Molten Magnesium Chloride (MgCl₂)

Molten MgCl₂ contains Mg²⁺ cations and Cl⁻ anions. The process is similar to NaCl.

🧪 Electrolyte: Molten MgCl₂
🔌 Electrodes: Inert (carbon)
🌡️ M.P.: 714°C
Cathode (Reduction) Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Mg
Anode (Oxidation) 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻
Overall Equation MgCl₂(l) → Mg(l) + Cl₂(g)
⊖ CATHODE

Magnesium Metal

🪙 Silvery metal deposited

Mg²⁺ ions gain 2 electrons and are reduced to magnesium metal.

⊕ ANODE

Chlorine Gas

💨 Greenish-yellow pungent gas

Cl⁻ ions lose 1 electron each and are oxidised to chlorine gas.

🧱 Electrolysis of Molten Calcium Oxide (CaO)

Molten CaO contains Ca²⁺ cations and O²⁻ anions.

🧪 Electrolyte: Molten CaO
🔌 Electrodes: Inert (carbon)
🌡️ M.P.: 2613°C
Cathode (Reduction) Ca²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Ca
Anode (Oxidation) 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻
Overall Equation 2CaO(l) → 2Ca(l) + O₂(g)
📝 Note: The extremely high melting point of CaO (2613°C) makes this process impractical and very energy-intensive. This example is mainly for understanding the principle.

🎯 Key Points for Exams

Make sure you can confidently explain these concepts

The ions must be free to move so they can migrate to the electrodes when a voltage is applied. In the solid state, ions are held in a fixed lattice and cannot move, so the substance cannot conduct electricity.

Cations are positively charged ions. They are attracted to the cathode which is the negative electrode (connected to the negative terminal of the battery). Opposite charges attract.

At the cathode: Reduction occurs — cations gain electrons from the electrode.
At the anode: Oxidation occurs — anions lose electrons to the electrode.

Remember: AN OX (Anode = Oxidation) and RED CAT (Reduction = Cathode).

At the high operating temperature (~950°C), the oxygen gas produced at the anode reacts with the carbon electrodes to form carbon dioxide:

C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)

This gradually burns away the anodes, so they need periodic replacement.

Cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) acts as a solvent for aluminium oxide. It lowers the melting point from 2072°C to about 950°C, which significantly reduces energy costs and makes the process economically viable.

In the external circuit (wires), current is carried by the flow of electrons.
In the electrolyte, current is carried by the movement of ions — cations move to the cathode and anions move to the anode.
