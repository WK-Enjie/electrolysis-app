📖 What is the Electrochemical Series?

A ranking of metals (and hydrogen) that determines which ions are preferentially discharged

🔑 Key Definition

The Electrochemical Series (ECS) is an arrangement of metals and hydrogen in order of their reactivity — their tendency to lose electrons (be oxidised). It is closely related to (and often presented as) the reactivity series. Higher in the series → metal is more reactive.

From the ion perspective:
Ions of metals higher in the ECS are more stable in solution — they are harder to discharge at the cathode.
Ions of metals lower in the ECS are less stable and have a greater tendency to gain electrons (be reduced). This is why they are preferentially discharged at the cathode during electrolysis.

In electrolysis, the ECS tells us which ion is preferentially discharged when two or more ions compete at an electrode.

📘 For deeper understanding: the actual Electrochemical Series

In scientific chemistry, the Electrochemical Series is defined by standard reduction potentials – it arranges elements (as ions) in order of their tendency to gain electrons (be reduced). This series is the reverse of the reactivity series shown above.

For example, in the true electrochemical series:
• Gold (Au³⁺) is at the top (greatest tendency to gain electrons).
• Potassium (K⁺) is at the bottom (lowest tendency to gain electrons).
• The rule for cathode discharge becomes: higher in the true series → easier to discharge.

At O Level, the term "Electrochemical Series" is often used interchangeably with the reactivity series, and the rule "lower in the series → easier to discharge" is taught for simplicity. Both approaches lead to the same correct predictions.

⊖ At the Cathode (Cations)

  • Cations of metals lower in the ECS are discharged first — their ions are less stable and gain electrons more readily
  • They accept electrons more easily → reduction
  • If the metal is below H (less reactive than hydrogen), the metal is deposited (e.g., Cu, Ag)
  • If the metal is above H (more reactive than hydrogen), hydrogen gas is produced instead

⊕ At the Anode (Anions)

  • Anions follow a separate discharge order
  • Halide ions (I⁻, Br⁻, Cl⁻) are discharged before OH⁻
  • SO₄²⁻ and NO₃⁻ are never discharged
  • Concentration can override position for halides
💡 Important Distinction — Metal vs Ion:
We compare the reactivity of metals, not their ions. For example, we say "copper is less reactive than hydrogen" (not "copper ion is less reactive than hydrogen ion"). Because copper the metal is less reactive, it sits below hydrogen in the ECS, and its ion (Cu²⁺) is less stable in solution — meaning Cu²⁺ has a greater tendency to gain electrons and be preferentially reduced at the cathode.

📜 Summary of Selective Discharge Rules

Four rules that determine products of aqueous electrolysis

⊖ Rule 1: Cathode — Position in ECS

The cation of the less reactive metal (lower in the ECS) is preferentially discharged. Its ion is less stable and has a greater tendency to gain electrons (be reduced).

Metal below H (less reactive than H): Metal deposited (e.g., Cu²⁺ → Cu)
Metal above H (more reactive than H): H₂ gas produced (H⁺ → H₂)

⊕ Rule 2: Anode — Anion Discharge Order

Halide ions (I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻) are discharged before OH⁻.

SO₄²⁻ and NO₃⁻ are never discharged.
When OH⁻ is discharged → O₂ gas produced.

⚗️ Rule 3: Concentration Effect

If a halide ion is in very high concentration, it may be preferentially discharged even over OH⁻.

Example: Conc. NaCl → Cl₂ at anode
Dilute NaCl → O₂ at anode

🔧 Rule 4: Active Anode

If the anode is made of an active (non-inert) metal, the anode itself dissolves instead of any anion being discharged.

Example: Cu anode in CuSO₄ → Cu dissolves
No gas produced; used in purification & electroplating.

🏆 Challenge: Apply Your Knowledge

Q1. In the electrolysis of aqueous copper(II) chloride with carbon electrodes, predict and explain the products at both electrodes.

Cathode: Copper is deposited. Copper is less reactive than hydrogen (Cu is lower than H in the ECS), so Cu²⁺ ions are less stable and are preferentially discharged over H⁺.
Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)

Anode: Chlorine gas is produced. Cl⁻ is a halide ion and is preferentially discharged before OH⁻.
2Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻

Q2. Explain why the products at the anode differ when electrolysing dilute NaCl vs concentrated NaCl, even though both contain the same ions.

Both solutions contain Cl⁻ and OH⁻ at the anode.

Dilute NaCl: Cl⁻ concentration is low. OH⁻ is preferentially discharged → O₂ gas produced.

Concentrated NaCl: Cl⁻ is in very high concentration. The concentration effect overrides the position rule, so Cl⁻ is preferentially discharged → Cl₂ gas produced.

Q3. During the electrolysis of CuSO₄(aq) with carbon electrodes, a student observes that the blue colour gradually fades. Explain why. What would happen if copper electrodes were used instead?

With carbon electrodes: Cu²⁺ ions are discharged at the cathode and deposited as Cu. Since the carbon anode is inert, it does not dissolve — no new Cu²⁺ enters the solution. Therefore Cu²⁺ concentration decreases and the blue colour fades.

With copper electrodes: Cu²⁺ is still deposited at the cathode, but the copper anode dissolves, releasing Cu²⁺ back into solution at the same rate. So Cu²⁺ concentration stays constant and the blue colour remains unchanged.

Q4. An object needs to be electroplated with nickel. State what the cathode, anode, and electrolyte should be. Write the half-equations at each electrode.

Cathode: The object to be plated
Anode: Pure nickel
Electrolyte: Nickel(II) sulphate — NiSO₄(aq)

Cathode equation: Ni²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Ni(s) (Reduction)
Anode equation: Ni(s) → Ni²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ (Oxidation)

Q5. In the electrolysis of aqueous sodium sulphate (Na₂SO₄) with carbon electrodes, predict the products at each electrode and write the overall equation.

Ions present: Na⁺, H⁺, SO₄²⁻, OH⁻

Cathode: Sodium is very reactive (high in ECS, more reactive than H). Na⁺ is very stable and not discharged. H⁺ is preferentially discharged → H₂ gas
2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g)

Anode: SO₄²⁻ is never discharged. OH⁻ is preferentially discharged → O₂ gas
4OH⁻(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g) + 4e⁻

Overall: 2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)
Same as electrolysis of water! Na₂SO₄ remains in solution.
