Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions

Why aqueous electrolysis is more complex: water provides extra ions! Master selective discharge for dilute and concentrated solutions.

🤔 Why Is Aqueous Electrolysis More Complex?

In aqueous solutions, there are extra ions from water competing for discharge

🔑 The Key Difference: Water Ionises!

When an ionic compound is dissolved in water, the solution contains ions from two sources:

1. Ions from the dissolved solute (e.g., Na⁺ and Cl⁻ from NaCl)
2. Ions from water itself: H⁺ (hydrogen ions) and OH⁻ (hydroxide ions)

This means there are competing ions at each electrode, and we must determine which ions are preferentially discharged.

💡 Key equation: H₂O(l) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)
This means in any aqueous electrolysis, H⁺ and OH⁻ ions are always present alongside the solute ions.

⚖️ Selective Discharge of Ions

When competing ions are present, which ion gets discharged? These rules decide.

📜 The Rules of Selective Discharge

When two or more ions compete for discharge at an electrode, the ion that is preferentially discharged depends on:

1. Position in electrochemical series — ions lower in the series are discharged more easily
2. Concentration of ions — if a particular ion is in very high concentration, it may be preferentially discharged even if it is higher in the series

⊖ At the Cathode (−)

Cations compete — which gets reduced first?

↓ Easier to discharge (lower reactivity)

  • 1 Ag⁺ — Silver (easiest)
  • 2 Cu²⁺ — Copper
  • 3 H⁺ — Hydrogen (from water)
  • 4 Pb²⁺ — Lead
  • 5 Fe²⁺ — Iron
  • 6 Zn²⁺ — Zinc
  • 7 Al³⁺ — Aluminium
  • 8 Mg²⁺ — Magnesium
  • 9 Na⁺ — Sodium
  • 10 Ca²⁺ — Calcium
  • 11 K⁺ — Potassium (hardest)
↑ Harder to discharge (higher reactivity)
📌 Metals below hydrogen in reactivity → the metal is deposited
Metals above hydrogen in reactivity → hydrogen gas is produced
VS

⊕ At the Anode (+)

Anions compete — which gets oxidised first?

↓ Easier to discharge

  • 1 I⁻ — Iodide (easiest)
  • 2 Br⁻ — Bromide
  • 3 Cl⁻ — Chloride
  • 4 OH⁻ — Hydroxide (from water)
  • 5 NO₃⁻ — Nitrate (hardest)
  • 6 SO₄²⁻ — Sulphate (hardest)
↑ Harder to discharge
📌 Halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) are discharged before OH⁻
SO₄²⁻ and NO₃⁻ are never discharged → OH⁻ is discharged instead → O₂ gas

⚠️ The Concentration Exception

If a particular ion is present in very high concentration, it may be preferentially discharged even though the rules above suggest otherwise.

Key example: In concentrated NaCl (brine), Cl⁻ ions are discharged at the anode instead of OH⁻ because Cl⁻ is in much higher concentration. But in dilute NaCl, OH⁻ is discharged instead, producing O₂.

⚗️ Electrolysis of Dilute Sulphuric Acid (H₂SO₄)

This is essentially the electrolysis of water, since dilute H₂SO₄ provides the H⁺ and SO₄²⁻ ions, and water provides H⁺ and OH⁻.

🧪 Electrolyte: Dilute H₂SO₄(aq)
🔌 Electrodes: Carbon/Platinum (inert)

Ions Present in Solution

H⁺from H₂SO₄
H⁺from H₂O
SO₄²⁻from H₂SO₄
OH⁻from H₂O

⊖ Cathode (−) Analysis

  • Cations present: H⁺ (from acid) and H⁺ (from water)
  • Only H⁺ is present — no competition
  • Product: Hydrogen gas (H₂)
  • Observation: Colourless gas bubbles, 'pop' test with lighted splint
Cathode (Reduction) 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g)

⊕ Anode (+) Analysis

  • Anions present: SO₄²⁻ and OH⁻
  • SO₄²⁻ is very hard to discharge (never discharged)
  • OH⁻ is selectively discharged
  • Product: Oxygen gas (O₂)
  • Observation: Colourless gas, relights a glowing splint
Anode (Oxidation) 4OH⁻(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g) + 4e⁻
Overall Equation 2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)
✅ Volume ratio: H₂ : O₂ = 2 : 1 (twice as much hydrogen is produced). The H₂SO₄ concentration increases over time because water is consumed but the acid is not.

💧 Electrolysis of Dilute Sodium Chloride Solution

In dilute NaCl, Cl⁻ concentration is low, so the concentration rule does NOT override the position rule.

Ions Present in Dilute NaCl(aq)

Na⁺from NaCl
H⁺from H₂O
Cl⁻from NaCl
OH⁻from H₂O

⊖ Cathode (−) Analysis

  • Competing cations: Na⁺ and H⁺
  • Na is more reactive than hydrogen (higher in ECS)
  • H⁺ is selectively discharged (lower in ECS = easier to reduce)
  • Product: Hydrogen gas (H₂)
Cathode (Reduction) 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g)

⊕ Anode (+) Analysis

  • Competing anions: Cl⁻ and OH⁻
  • In dilute solution, Cl⁻ concentration is low
  • OH⁻ is selectively discharged
  • Product: Oxygen gas (O₂)
Anode (Oxidation) 4OH⁻(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g) + 4e⁻
Overall — same as electrolysis of water! 2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)
💡 Key insight: The Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions remain in solution — the solution becomes more concentrated NaCl (and eventually NaOH forms) as water is consumed. The products are the same as electrolysis of water!

🧂 Electrolysis of Concentrated NaCl (Brine)

This is a very important industrial process! When the Cl⁻ concentration is high, it overrides the position rule.

Ions in Concentrated NaCl(aq) — Brine

Na⁺HIGH conc.
H⁺low conc.
Cl⁻HIGH conc.
OH⁻low conc.

⚠️ Size represents relative concentration — solute ions vastly outnumber water ions

⊖ Cathode (−) Analysis

  • Competing cations: Na⁺ and H⁺
  • Na is more reactive — H⁺ is still selectively discharged
  • Concentration does NOT affect cathode for active metals
  • Product: Hydrogen gas (H₂)
Cathode (Reduction) 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g)

⊕ Anode (+) Analysis

  • Competing anions: Cl⁻ (HIGH conc.) and OH⁻
  • Concentration effect! Cl⁻ is in very high concentration
  • Cl⁻ is preferentially discharged (overrides position rule)
  • Product: Chlorine gas (Cl₂)
  • Observation: Greenish-yellow gas, pungent smell, bleaches litmus
Anode (Oxidation) 2Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻
Overall Equation 2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g) + Cl₂(g)
✅ Three useful products from brine:
🫧 Hydrogen — fuel, making margarine
🟡 Chlorine — water purification, making bleach, PVC
🧴 Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) — soaps, paper, detergents

🔵 Electrolysis of CuSO₄(aq) with Carbon Electrodes

Copper is below hydrogen in the electrochemical series, so Cu²⁺ is preferentially discharged at the cathode instead of H⁺.

🧪 Electrolyte: CuSO₄(aq)
🔌 Electrodes: Carbon (inert)

Ions Present

Cu²⁺from CuSO₄
H⁺from H₂O
SO₄²⁻from CuSO₄
OH⁻from H₂O

⊖ Cathode (−)

  • Competing: Cu²⁺ and H⁺
  • Cu is below H in ECS (less reactive)
  • Cu²⁺ is selectively discharged
  • Product: Copper metal deposited
  • Observation: Reddish-brown solid on cathode
Cathode (Reduction) Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)

⊕ Anode (+)

  • Competing: SO₄²⁻ and OH⁻
  • SO₄²⁻ is never discharged
  • OH⁻ is selectively discharged
  • Product: Oxygen gas (O₂)
Anode (Oxidation) 4OH⁻(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g) + 4e⁻
⚠️ Important observations over time:
• The blue colour fades as Cu²⁺ ions are removed from solution
• The solution becomes more acidic (H₂SO₄ left behind)
• Eventually when all Cu²⁺ is used up, H⁺ is discharged instead → H₂ gas appears

🟤 Electrolysis of CuSO₄(aq) with Copper Electrodes

This is completely different! When the anode is made of copper (an active metal), the anode itself dissolves. This is the basis of copper purification.

🧪 Electrolyte: CuSO₄(aq)
🔌 Electrodes: Copper (active!)

⊖ Cathode (−) — Pure Copper

  • Cu²⁺ is selectively discharged (same as with carbon)
  • Product: Pure copper deposited on cathode
  • Observation: Cathode gains mass (gets thicker)
Cathode (Reduction) Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)

⊕ Anode (+) — Impure Copper

  • The copper anode dissolves!
  • Copper atoms lose electrons and enter solution as Cu²⁺
  • OH⁻ and SO₄²⁻ are NOT discharged
  • Observation: Anode loses mass (gets thinner)
Anode (Oxidation) Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻
✅ Key features with copper electrodes:
• The blue colour stays the same — Cu²⁺ removed at cathode is replaced by Cu²⁺ from anode
• Concentration of CuSO₄ remains constant
• Copper is effectively transferred from anode to cathode
• No gas is produced at either electrode
• This is how copper purification works (see Applications topic)

⚡ Carbon vs Copper Electrodes — Side by Side

Feature Carbon Electrodes (Inert) Copper Electrodes (Active)
Cathode product Copper deposited Copper deposited (pure)
Anode product Oxygen gas (O₂) Anode dissolves (Cu → Cu²⁺)
Solution colour Blue fades over time Blue stays the same
Cu²⁺ concentration Decreases Stays constant
Gas produced? Yes — O₂ at anode No gas
Electrode mass Cathode gains Cu; Anode unchanged Cathode gains mass; Anode loses mass
Application Copper extraction from solution Purification of copper

🎯 Key Exam Points — Aqueous Electrolysis

Step 1: Write the ions from the dissolved solute (e.g., NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻)
Step 2: Always add H⁺ and OH⁻ from water
Step 3: List all cations together (for cathode) and all anions together (for anode)

The concentration rule mainly applies to halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) at the anode. When the halide ion is in concentrated solution, it is preferentially discharged over OH⁻. In dilute solution, OH⁻ is discharged instead.

At O-Level: Concentration effect is usually tested with NaCl (dilute vs concentrated).

When the anode is made of an active metal (e.g., copper), the anode itself dissolves instead of ions being discharged. The metal atoms lose electrons and enter solution as ions:

Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻

This means: anode loses mass, cathode gains mass, solution colour stays the same, no gas is produced.

The blue colour is due to Cu²⁺ ions in solution. With carbon (inert) electrodes, Cu²⁺ ions are discharged at the cathode and deposited as copper metal. Since the carbon anode does not dissolve, no new Cu²⁺ ions are added. Therefore the concentration of Cu²⁺ decreases and the blue colour fades.

When OH⁻ ions are discharged at the anode, the equation is:

4OH⁻(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g) + 4e⁻

Note: This equation shows that 4 electrons are lost (not 2). Make sure the equation is balanced — 4OH⁻ gives O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻.

SO₄²⁻ and NO₃⁻ are very stable polyatomic ions. They require too much energy to be oxidised. Therefore, OH⁻ (from water) is always discharged in preference, producing oxygen gas. At O-Level, you can simply state: "SO₄²⁻ / NO₃⁻ ions are too difficult to discharge."
