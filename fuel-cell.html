🌱 What is a Hydrogen Fuel Cell?

A fuel cell generates electricity from a chemical reaction without combustion

🔑 Key Definition

A hydrogen fuel cell is an electrochemical device that converts the chemical energy of hydrogen and oxygen directly into electrical energy, with water as the only product.

Unlike a battery, a fuel cell does not run down or need recharging — it will continue to produce electricity as long as hydrogen and oxygen are supplied.

✅ Clean energy: The only by-product is water — no carbon dioxide, no pollutants, no greenhouse gases! This makes hydrogen fuel cells a promising technology for clean, sustainable energy.

⚙️ How Does a Fuel Cell Work?

Step-by-step process inside a hydrogen-oxygen fuel cell

STEP 1 ⛽

Hydrogen Enters

H₂ gas is fed into the negative electrode (anode of the fuel cell). Each H₂ molecule loses electrons and forms H⁺ ions.

STEP 2 ⚡

Electrons Flow

The released electrons travel through the external circuit from the anode to the cathode, creating an electric current.

STEP 3 🌬️

Oxygen Enters

O₂ gas (from air) is fed into the positive electrode (cathode). It gains the electrons that arrived through the wire.

STEP 4 💧

Water Forms

At the cathode, O₂ combines with H⁺ ions (that migrated through the membrane) and electrons to form water — the only product.

⚡ Hydrogen Fuel Cell — Animated Diagram

Overview

The hydrogen fuel cell has two electrodes separated by an electrolyte membrane. H₂ enters at the anode, O₂ enters at the cathode. H₂ is oxidised, releasing electrons that flow through the external circuit to power a device. H⁺ ions migrate through the membrane to combine with O₂ and electrons at the cathode, forming water.

Anode — Oxidation of Hydrogen 2H₂(g) → 4H⁺(aq) + 4e⁻
Cathode — Reduction of Oxygen O₂(g) + 4H⁺(aq) + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O(l)
Overall Equation 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)

⚡ Fuel Cell vs Simple Cell vs Combustion

Understanding how a fuel cell differs from other energy sources

🌱

Hydrogen Fuel Cell

Chemical → Electrical (directly)

Fuel: H₂ + O₂

Product: Water only

Pollutants: None ✓

Runs out? No — continuous supply

🔋

Simple Cell / Battery

Chemical → Electrical

Fuel: Reactive metals

Product: Metal ions + H₂

Pollutants: Metal waste

Runs out? Yes — metals consumed

🔥

Combustion of H₂

Chemical → Heat (→ Electrical via turbine)

Fuel: H₂ + O₂

Product: Water + heat

Pollutants: None (but less efficient)

Efficiency: ~30-40% (energy lost as heat)

📝 Why is a fuel cell more efficient than combustion?
In combustion, chemical energy is first converted to heat, then to mechanical energy, then to electrical energy — with large losses at each step (~30-40% efficient).
In a fuel cell, chemical energy converts directly to electrical energy — much more efficient (~60-80%!).

✅❌ Advantages & Disadvantages

Important for exam questions: "State advantages and disadvantages of hydrogen fuel cells"

✅ Advantages

🌿
Clean energy: Only product is water — no CO₂, no pollutants, no greenhouse gases
⚡
High efficiency: Converts chemical energy directly to electrical energy (~60-80% efficient)
♻️
Renewable fuel: Hydrogen can be produced from water by electrolysis using renewable energy
🔋
No recharging needed: Continues to work as long as fuel (H₂ and O₂) is supplied
🔇
Silent operation: No moving parts (unlike combustion engines), so very quiet
🌍
Abundant fuel: Hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe

❌ Disadvantages

💰
High cost: Fuel cells require expensive platinum catalysts
🏭
H₂ production: Most hydrogen is currently made from fossil fuels (steam reforming of methane), which produces CO₂
💥
Storage difficulty: H₂ is highly flammable and explosive — needs special high-pressure tanks or cryogenic storage
⛽
Lack of infrastructure: Very few hydrogen refuelling stations compared to petrol stations
⚡
Energy to produce H₂: Electrolysis of water requires large amounts of electricity — not efficient if electricity comes from fossil fuels
📦
Bulky storage: Hydrogen gas has low energy density by volume — needs large tanks
💡 Exam tip: When asked for advantages, always mention: (1) only water produced (no pollution), (2) high efficiency. When asked for disadvantages, always mention: (1) H₂ production from fossil fuels still causes pollution, (2) storage and safety issues, (3) high cost of platinum catalyst.

🚀 Real-World Applications

Where hydrogen fuel cells are being used today and in the future

🚗

Fuel Cell Vehicles

Toyota Mirai, Hyundai Nexo — cars powered by H₂ fuel cells with ~500km range

🚌

Public Transport

Hydrogen buses in London, Beijing, and other cities for zero-emission transit

🚀

Space Missions

NASA used H₂ fuel cells in Apollo and Space Shuttle programs for electricity and drinking water

🏢

Backup Power

Hospitals, data centres, and telecom towers use fuel cells for reliable backup electricity

🚢

Marine Vessels

Submarines and cargo ships exploring H₂ power for silent, clean propulsion

🏠

Home Power

Japan's "ENE-FARM" — residential fuel cells providing electricity and hot water for homes

📂 Key Exam Points

Anode (−) — Oxidation:
2H₂(g) → 4H⁺(aq) + 4e⁻

Cathode (+) — Reduction:
O₂(g) + 4H⁺(aq) + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O(l)

Overall:
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)

The only product of the fuel cell reaction is water. There are no carbon dioxide emissions, no pollutant gases (like SO₂ or NO₂), and no particulates. However, the production of hydrogen may still cause pollution if fossil fuels are used.

They are reverse processes!

Fuel cell: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O + electricity (chemical → electrical)
Electrolysis: 2H₂O + electricity → 2H₂ + O₂ (electrical → chemical)

In a fuel cell, hydrogen and oxygen combine to produce water and electricity. In electrolysis, water is decomposed using electricity into hydrogen and oxygen.

For hydrogen fuel cells to become widespread, we need to solve:
(1) Clean H₂ production — using renewable energy for electrolysis of water
(2) Safe storage — high-pressure tanks or metal hydrides
(3) Distribution — building H₂ refuelling stations
(4) Cost reduction — making fuel cells cheaper (reduce platinum use)
